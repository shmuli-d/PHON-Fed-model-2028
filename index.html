Could One Nation form government at the next federal election?

An interactive psephological model · built on the May 2026 RedBridge / Accent MRP, Bonham aggregates & Antony Green seat geography · horizon: the next election (~2028) · "win" = form government

Calibrated headline estimate (derived in §6)
~16% P(One Nation wins majority government OR leads a conservative minority government) at the next election, absent effective intervention
plausible range 11–22% · roughly a 1-in-6 shot (raw model ~18%; nudged down for coalition-formation frictions)

The number is not ~1% (the naive "minor party" prior) and not 50%. It sits in between for one reason: a genuine right-of-centre realignment is underway — ON is already on 28–31% primary and the Coalition has collapsed — but even the most ON-favourable credible model has ON becoming the Opposition, not the government. The question reduces to a single uncertain variable: does the surge keep compounding to ~34%+, or does it deflate before 2028?

1 · What actually happened (the trajectory)

In May 2025 One Nation won 6.4% of the House primary vote. By June 2026 it had reached ~28% (aggregate), with individual polls at 31% (Newspoll, 8 Jun) — first on primaries, ahead of Labor, with the Coalition crushed to ~18–21%.

Kevin Bonham notes the early surge "eerily paralleled" the Voice No campaign's acceleration, stalled Feb–Apr, then re-accelerated after the March Budget (rated among the worst-received ever) and ON's historic Farrer by-election win.

Aggregate line is indicative (Morgan/Newspoll/Redbridge/YouGov); the dot marks the 8 Jun Newspoll. The critical fact for everything below: on two-party-preferred, Labor still leads (Bonham: Labor 52.4 vs Coalition; 52.9 vs One Nation head-to-head). Primary-vote leadership ≠ winning.

1b · Leading indicator: membership & ground game

One Nation now claims 60,000–70,000 paid members — more than Labor (~55k) or the Liberals (~50k, incl. LNP) — after 40,000+ sign-ups since the 2025 election, plus 100+ new branches (targeting one per seat) and Gina Rinehart's public backing (AFR, 5 Jun 2026).

Why this matters as a leading indicator: branch density and dues-paying membership convert into booth coverage, scrutineers, a candidate pipeline and a doorknocking base — the ground-game capacity PHON lacked in past surges (1998, 2017). It is the clearest quantitative sign this operation is more durable and professional than its predecessors, and it underwrites the "realignment world."

Not directly comparable (shown for scale): the Greens ~15k members (2020, most recent published figure — dated); the teals are not a membership party (Climate 200 raised ~$6m in 2022 with roughly 1,000 volunteers per target seat). Per your steer, this indicator is not fed into the headline weightings — it is corroborating context only.

The engine powering ON's rise — Coalition preferences flowing to One Nation — is well-supported (SA 67%, 2025 federal 74.5%, Farrer ~65%; my 70% sits in the middle). The subtler question is the Greens firewall. In SA, Greens leaked 19.6% to One Nation in Labor-vs-ON contests but 15.5% to the Liberals in Labor-vs-Liberal contests. The per-seat data attributes that gap to seat composition rather than party: progressive ALP-vs-ON seats (Badcoe 91.5% to Labor, West Torrens 88%) held Labor's vote better than some ALP-vs-Lib seats, while the leak spikes only in conservative outer-suburban ON seats (Elizabeth 67%, Reynell 73%), where Greens voters are themselves more right-leaning. Conservative seats leak rightward more whoever the right-wing candidate is.

Each seat's Greens→Labor rate is therefore a function of its conservatism (≈70% in the most conservative seats, ≈97% inner-metro), the same whether the opponent is ON or Liberal. The headline is insensitive to this modelling choice — a flat firewall gives ~17% — because the decisive ON-vs-Labor seats are conservative on either treatment. The firewall is the biggest swing factor: at the default (avg ~91%) the headline is ~16%; if federal Greens behave like SA's composition curve (avg ~82%), it rises to ~25% and Labor loses its majority even at today's vote. Drag the "Greens firewall" slider in §2 down toward 82% to see it. The default sits above SA because the federal Farrer byelection did not reproduce the leak, the MRP anchors higher, and part of SA's leak was donkey-vote driven.

3 · The strongest case FOR and AGAINST

Strongest case FOR (steelman)

  1. A self-reinforcing realignment. 6.4% → ~30% in 13 months, re-accelerating post-Budget — a trajectory that reads as structural rather than a one-off protest spike. Compounding processes are systematically under-estimated; the default is continued acceleration unless a specific decelerating force is named.
  2. The Coalition is being annihilated as a brand. The MRP projects the Coalition winning zero seats in QLD, WA, SA and Tasmania. When the institutional centre-right alternative dies, ON inherits the "alternative government" slot — as Reform did to the UK Tories, Reform/Canadian-PC in 1993, RN in France.
  3. Preferences are normalising in ON's favour. Coalition→ON already 74.5% in 2025 and the Coalition still puts ON above Labor on how-to-vote cards. A consolidating right bloc lifts ON's 2PP.
  4. Complementary geographies (Bonham's own caveat). If Coalition and ON stop splitting the right vote and instead carve complementary turf, Labor's "safe" 52% 2PP can crack faster than uniform-swing intuition suggests.
  5. Labor's win rests on a soft 31% primary. A government governing off ~31% in a fragmented field is structurally fragile; a recession/stagflation shock by 2028 could collapse it.
  6. An organised machine this time. ON now claims 60–70k paid members (more than either major), 100+ branches, Rinehart backing and an overhauled comms operation (§1b) — the booth/scrutineer/candidate capacity it has always lacked. Past surges deflated partly for want of exactly this.
  7. The firewall may leak. Real-world SA preferences sent ~20% of Greens votes to ON (up to 33% in one seat) — the "screw the system" crossover (§2b). If that holds federally, ON's seat conversion is materially higher than the MRP assumes.
  8. "Without intervention" removes the brakes. The question explicitly assumes no effective, well-resourced counter-mobilisation — i.e. the compounding default runs unchecked for 24 months.

Strongest case AGAINST

  1. Even the most ON-favourable credible model makes ON the Opposition, not the government. RedBridge MRP at ON 28%: Labor 76 (majority), ON 53, Coalition 12. Antony Green: "the only party with any chance of 76 is ALP."
  2. Vote efficiency collapses past ~55 seats. ON's vote is concentrated in regional QLD/NSW/WA + outer-metro. The next 20 seats to reach 76 are inner/suburban Labor strongholds where ON polls 15–25% and loses on Green+Labor preferences. Diminishing returns are brutal.
  3. Preferential voting is still a firewall — just a leakier one than it looks. Greens preferences favour Labor over ON, but in the conservative seats where ON actually competes that's only ~80% (not the ~90% inner-metro rate), and moderate-Liberal preferences also break partly to Labor. Shadow-2PP: Labor 52.9 vs ON. ON still needs a large primary to overcome it — the firewall holds, but the SA-observed leakage (§2b) means it holds less firmly than the headline 90% suggests.
  4. There is no arithmetic path to 76 for a right bloc. ON 53 + Coalition 12 + KAP 1 = 66. The crossbench is mostly teal independents who would back Labor over ON. ON can only govern if Labor also collapses into the low 60s.
  5. Base rate ≈ zero. No minor/third party has ever formed federal government in Australia. Compulsory + preferential voting has protected the two-major system for a century (a stabilised equilibrium).
  6. Candidate quality & in-fighting — the real internal risk. Leadership exit is low: actuarially ~6% over the window (she is 72, not retiring, and her recruits have no interest in pushing her out). The historic PHON vulnerability is thin candidate quality and implosion under scrutiny (1998 "Ezytax") — which bites harder when you must field ~150 credible candidates and survive policy costing. Mitigant this time: a more professional, better-funded, more strategic operation than past surges, so this drag may be weaker than history implies.
  7. Polls 2 years out overstate. Mid-term minor-party surges typically deflate once "politics turns serious" and costed policy is demanded. Governments usually recover ground.

4 · Probability by world-state (the real uncertainty)

At horizons beyond ~2 years, individual-event forecasting gives way to distributions over world-states. The headline number is a weighted blend of three futures, each run through the full two-stage Monte Carlo (trajectory uncertainty × Hanson illness/mortality risk ≈6% × preference risk × seat noise).

Mechanical blend ≈ 18%; displayed ~16% after a small haircut for coalition-formation frictions the seat engine ignores (the Coalition remnant agreeing to serve under Hanson; the teal crossbench installing Labor rather than ON even when the right bloc nominally reaches 76). Two big swing factors: (1) the Greens firewall — at SA-calibrated (composition) leakage the headline is ~25% (§2b); (2) how much weight the "realignment continues" world deserves — reasonable analysts could weight it 0.10–0.30, and a more professional, better-funded PHON operation argues for weighting it higher. Those judgments — not the seat mechanics — are where the action is.

Note the headline question and the most likely dramatic event diverge: the median surprise of this period is not "ON wins" — it is "ON destroys and replaces the Coalition as the Opposition while Labor keeps governing." That is itself a historic realignment, and it is far more likely than ON taking power.

6 · Calibration, sanity checks & known unknowns

Calibration against the anchors

Is "ON forms government in 2028" as likely as rolling a 1 on a d6 (≈17%)? About the same. ON is already at 31% in one poll, the government-threshold is ~34%, and the Greens firewall leaks more than the MRP assumes — so this is not a deck-of-100 tail. But the partner arithmetic, the 24-month deflation risk and coalition-formation friction keep it around one-in-six. ~16% survives the anchor check.

Bucket sanity check

If I make ~6 distinct ~16% claims of this kind across a full-world forecast, I should expect ~1 to occur. Would I be shocked if "ON forms government" specifically came true? Surprised, not shocked — consistent with a ~16% bucket. If I'd called it 1%, I'd be too confident given a live 31% primary; if 35%, I'd be ignoring the partner arithmetic and base rate.

What I think I'm missing (known unknowns)

  • Coalition strategic choice. A Liberal–National split, a merger with ON, or a formal right alliance would rewrite the preference and seat maths entirely — in either direction.
  • Hanson succession. Hanson's exit before the election is unlikely (~6% over the window from actuarial mortality/illness risk given that Hanson is 72), but the conditional vote-hit if it happened is potentially high — snap polling implies a ~20–25% cut in that case.
  • Economic regime by 2028. A recession/stagflation or AI-driven unemployment shock is the most plausible single accelerant of incumbent collapse — a cross-domain channel from economics to politics.
  • Redistributions. The 2026–27 boundary redraws (e.g. Wright, Capricornia, Longman) shift specific targets.
  • Senate. Modelled here only for the House. ON's near-certain Senate gains (potentially the balance of power) are a separate, higher-probability story.
  • Poll error / herding. Several pollsters now decline to publish 2PP; the primary-vote surge may be partly a methods artefact (Bonham's caution).
  • Non-uniform swing. This model uses a uniform points-swing baseline; a true realignment would swing non-uniformly, which could be more or less efficient than modelled.
